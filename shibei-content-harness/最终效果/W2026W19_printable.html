AI Schools: A New Model for Higher Education Reform
China Daily | technology

In recent years, approximately one hundred universities in China have collaborated with enterprises and research institutes to establish schools dedicated to artificial intelligence, marking a significant step toward fostering AI talents and driving higher education reform.

Three prominent differences distinguish these new AI schools from traditional university departments. First, their training orientation focuses on real-world business problems and socioeconomic needs rather than conventional disciplinary teaching. Second, AI schools allocate resources specifically for computing power, data, and industrial projects, which often demands unconventional investment, whereas traditional departments must balance resource distribution across multiple disciplines. Third, AI schools operate with more flexible management mechanisms and higher decision-making efficiency.

By breaking down disciplinary barriers, deepening industry-university-research integration, and optimizing personalized vocational training, AI schools provide a distinctive model for talent cultivation in cutting-edge fields such as original research and scientific application. Under the cooperation framework, universities handle student recruitment and academic degree approval, while AI schools take charge of curriculum design and research practice.

However, discrepancies in management systems and academic cultures between universities and AI schools lead to inefficiencies in communication and resource allocation, and high coordination costs hinder curriculum and practice integration. Furthermore, unimplemented mechanisms for the transformation of achievements and benefit distribution affect the enthusiasm of teachers and students, making it difficult to meet the personalized training needs of students with interdisciplinary backgrounds.

Universities seek preferential treatment in resource allocation from educational departments to expand undergraduate admissions and attract high-quality students. They also need resource-sharing policies to establish long-term mechanisms for sharing computing power and data resources, which would reduce access barriers for teachers and students and ultimately improve the integrated system for talent cultivation.

Nature as Infrastructure: The Rise of Nature-Based Solutions
China Daily | environment

Climate change is no longer a distant warning, as melting glaciers, dying coral reefs and collapsing ecosystems are racing toward dangerous tipping points that pose significant risks to both people and nature. In response, many countries including China are treating nature as part of their infrastructure, recognizing that investing in ecosystems could strengthen resilience against environmental threats.

Nature-based solutions (NbS) turn this idea into practice by protecting, restoring and managing ecosystems so that they continue to provide essential services for people and businesses. Forests regulate water and stabilize slopes, wetlands store floodwater and filter pollution, and mangroves shield coasts from storms and erosion. Urban trees and parks cool streets and reduce heat stress. In these ways, nature delivers ecosystem services — flood protection, water security, coastal defense and urban cooling — that make existing grey infrastructure more resilient. Unlike roads, dams or drainage systems, natural systems can renew themselves and deliver multiple benefits simultaneously.

Yet investment in nature still falls far short of what is needed, with global estimates indicating an annual biodiversity finance gap of approximately 700 billion dollars between now and 2030. One major barrier is that many of nature's benefits, such as a stable climate or cleaner air, are not properly valued and priced in project appraisals or national accounts. A holistic approach is therefore needed to align policy, project preparation and finance, so that NbS are systematically built into decisions rather than added on later.

Across emerging markets and developing countries, strong examples of NbS are already emerging. In China, for instance, a solar farm built on once-degraded land demonstrates how raised panels allow sheep to graze in their shade while slowing wind erosion, restoring ground cover biodiversity, storing carbon and providing local herders with steadier incomes alongside clean power. As the world looks toward the 31st United Nations Climate Change Conference, the focus is shifting from high-level commitments to concrete implementation, making this the right moment to bring NbS into the mainstream of climate and development plans.

New Regulation Redefines the Role of Pharmaceutical Representatives
China Daily | health

China has recently issued a regulation targeting the conduct of pharmaceutical representatives, aiming to crack down on illicit practices by strictly limiting their role to academic information sharing rather than sales-driven activities. According to the regulation released by the National Medical Products Administration and six other government departments, these representatives are responsible for providing physicians with product information and must never offer commercial bribes, which severely disrupt fair competition and the healthy development of the pharmaceutical industry.

The new regulation, which will take effect on August 1, 2026, requires pharmaceutical representatives to hold at least an associate degree in medicine, pharmacy, or a related field and possess knowledge of clinical pharmacology, while also specifying a clearly defined area of operation when registering. It explicitly prohibits representatives from engaging in sales-related activities, including collecting payments, handling purchase and sales invoices, or offering donations and subsidies to medical institutions based on drug sales volume. Additionally, representatives must not exaggerate therapeutic effects, conceal known adverse reaction information, or illegally collect and disseminate patient data.

Pharmaceutical companies are forbidden from employing or authorizing representatives found to have engaged in commercial bribery, and medical institutions are prohibited from communicating with unregistered representatives or accepting benefits from them in any form. Disciplinary measures, including public disclosure of violations, restrictions on participation in drug procurement programs, and limitations on national medical insurance services, will be enforced to strengthen oversight and deter future misconduct.

Professor Yu Wei from the research center for healthcare policy and management at Shanghai University of Finance and Economics noted that the regulation signals a shift in the responsibilities of pharmaceutical representatives from driving sales to communicating academic medical information only. Yu stated that representatives will no longer be able to rely on personal connections to influence prescriptions and must instead create value through authentic, standardized, and traceable medical information services. In the long term, these changes will benefit the profession and help innovative pharmaceutical companies establish a healthier and more credible academic promotion system.

Enduring Protection for Workers in Emerging Fields
China Daily | society

On April 26, the Chinese government issued the first national-level policy document specifically addressing the needs of workers in new forms of employment, including food delivery riders, couriers, ride-hailing drivers, and live-streamers who earn their living through digital platforms. This landmark document aims to close a long-standing institutional gap by formally bringing this emerging workforce into the national services and governance system.

China's social security system was developed alongside industrialization and remains largely based on the work unit model, under which traditional workers sign formal labor contracts with fixed employers, contribute to social insurance, and receive corresponding benefits. However, workers in new forms of employment face considerable ambiguity because their flexible arrangements, which often involve platforms, outsourcing firms, and franchisees, create complex employment relationships that leave them without clear legal protection in areas such as pay, work injury insurance, and rest rights.

The guidelines require platform companies to use labor contracts suited to the sector, define rights and obligations lawfully, set remuneration in line with workload, pay wages in full and on time, and ensure workers' right to rest with enhanced protection during extreme weather. Additionally, platforms must improve transparency and guarantee workers' rights to be informed about, participate in, and make choices regarding algorithmic rules that govern their work.

On the public services front, basic services should be provided based on place of residence rather than household registration, allowing services to follow the flow of workers. Measures include improving social security systems for flexible employment, facilitating the transfer of social insurance, expanding pilot programs for occupational injury protection, and exploring the inclusion of flexible workers in the housing provident fund.

These guidelines emerge as China's population structure and the platform economy reshape employment patterns, shifting the social security system from one centered on work units to one centered on society at large. By standardizing employment practices, safeguarding entitlements, and opening pathways for career development, the guidelines lay a foundation for transforming jobs that were once temporary stopgaps into viable, stable careers for tens of millions of workers.
